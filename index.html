Orphaned Macaque Named "Trenchy" After the WW2 Trench Helmet That's Lived in Zoo Storage for Decades

A head keeper's obsession with military history and a dusty Brodie helmet found behind a filing cabinet have given one of Bristol's smallest residents a name that's captured the world's attention.

SR
By Siobhan Redmond  ·  Senior Features Writer
Published Tuesday, 17 March 2026, 09:14 GMT  ·  Updated 11:32 GMT

Deep inside a cluttered equipment room at Kestrel Valley Wildlife Park in Bishopsworth, Bristol, tucked beneath a broken sprinkler head and a bin bag full of old lanyards, sat a dented olive-green steel helmet. It had been there, according to the park's longest-serving groundskeeper, since "well before I started, and that were 1994." Nobody knew quite how it had arrived, or who it belonged to. Nobody, that is, until Graham Hartley decided to find out.

Hartley, 54, is Kestrel Valley's Head of Primate Care and, by his own description, "a completely insufferable WW2 bore." His colleagues confirm this affectionately. The helmet he pulled from the storeroom on a rainy Wednesday last November was a British Mk II Brodie — the classic steel bowl-shaped helmet issued to Allied troops during both World Wars, the kind that became synonymous with the image of a soldier hunkered in a trench somewhere in Belgium or northern France, rain coming sideways, mud up to his knees, waiting.

The inside of the rim bore a faded ink stamp: Pte. T. Webb, 4th Btn. Royal Fusiliers, 1917.

"I stood there in that storeroom holding it and I had this proper moment," Hartley told The Field Post. "This thing went through the Somme, most likely. Possibly Passchendaele. Private Webb wore it in conditions we can barely imagine — cold, alone, surrounded by chaos, completely vulnerable. And he got through."

The Mk II Brodie helmet bearing the inscription 'Pte. T. Webb, 4th Btn. Royal Fusiliers, 1917' is now displayed in a glass case inside Kestrel Valley's keeper room. Park historians believe it was donated to the grounds — then a farmstead used by the Home Guard — sometime in the early 1940s.

He didn't know it then, but that same afternoon, three enclosures across the park, a three-week-old Japanese macaque (Macaca fuscata) was fighting a very different kind of battle.

The infant, born on the 7th of November to a seven-year-old female named Hazel, had been rejected by its mother within hours of birth — a behavioural anomaly that, while distressing to witness, is not unheard of in captive primates under stress. With temperatures dropping overnight and the troop showing no signs of adopting the newborn, Hartley and his team made the call to bring the infant into the park's animal care unit for hand-rearing. The chances of survival were, he admits, "genuinely not great."

"I put that little helmet on the shelf above his incubator that night. I told him: Private Webb made it through the trenches. You can make it through this."

— Graham Hartley, Head of Primate Care, Kestrel Valley Wildlife Park

What followed was twelve days of round-the-clock feeding, warming, and constant monitoring. Hartley, who lives twenty minutes from the park, slept in the care unit on a camp bed for the first five nights. On day six, the infant opened his eyes properly for the first time and, in the words of keeper Eleanor Briggs, "grabbed Graham's finger and just held on." On day twelve, he was declared stable.

The helmet, which Hartley had brought down and placed on the shelf above the incubator on a whim, had never moved. "It sounds daft," he says, not entirely embarrassed, "but I put it there the first night. I said to him: 'Private Webb made it through the trenches. You can make it through this.'"

The name, when it came, was never really a question.

"Trenchy," Hartley says. "Because that's what those soldiers were — trench boys. Young lads dropped into something impossible and expected to survive it. That's what he was."

  • Currently 19 weeks old; being gradually reintegrated with the park's macaque troop
  • Kestrel Valley Wildlife Park is accredited by the British & Irish Association of Zoos and Aquariums (BIAZA)

The story might have stayed contained within Kestrel Valley's 84 acres were it not for a short video posted to the park's social media accounts in January — just forty-three seconds of Trenchy clinging to Hartley's forearm while the keeper narrated the story of the helmet, turning it slowly in his free hand as the monkey watched with what viewers universally described as "an unreasonably attentive expression." The video accumulated 14 million views in four days.

Keepers have begun a gradual reintegration process with the park's existing macaque troop, which currently numbers eleven individuals. Last week, Trenchy shared a supervised space with an eleven-year-old female named Biscuit — described by staff as "extraordinarily patient."

The response has been, by Hartley's admission, "completely baffling and rather lovely." Kestrel Valley has received over 4,000 adoption enquiries from thirty-one countries. The park's research team has been contacted by two universities seeking to document Trenchy's social reintegration for published studies. A Bristol-based illustrator named Priya Nair has announced a children's book, tentatively titled Trenchy and the Tin Hat, due for release in autumn 2026. The helmet has been assessed by Antiques Roadshow researchers and provisionally valued at between £3,800 and £4,600, but Hartley has made clear it will be going absolutely nowhere.

"That helmet stays here," he says flatly. "It's Trenchy's. Full stop."

For now, the focus is on the slow, careful work of reintegration. Japanese macaques are intensely social animals, and hand-reared individuals must be reintroduced to their troops with considerable care to avoid long-term behavioural and psychological consequences. Kestrel Valley's team is cautiously optimistic. Last week, for the first time, Trenchy was placed in a shared space with an eleven-year-old female named Biscuit, who is described by keepers as "extraordinarily patient" and has shown no signs of aggression toward him.

"He'll get there," Briggs says. "He's tough. He's a trench boy."

As for Private Thomas Webb of the 4th Battalion Royal Fusiliers — the young man whose name is stamped inside the helmet — the park is working with the Commonwealth War Graves Commission to trace his record. Early indications suggest he survived the war, was demobilised in 1919, and returned to his home in Stoke-on-Trent, where census records show him living as late as 1951.

He made it. And so, it seems, did Trenchy.

About Kestrel Valley

Kestrel Valley Wildlife Park is an 84-acre conservation zoo in Bishopsworth, Bristol, accredited by BIAZA and home to over 340 species. Founded in 1963, the park has an active breeding programme for eleven endangered species and receives approximately 580,000 visitors annually.
