The Search for Private Webb: Tracing the Soldier Behind Pip's Helmet

The faded ink stamp inside an olive-green steel helmet — Pte. T. Webb, 4th Btn. Royal Fusiliers, 1917 — set Graham Hartley on a months-long search through military archives, census records, and Commonwealth War Graves databases. He found him. And the story he uncovered is more remarkable than anyone expected.

DP
By Daniel Phipps  ·  History Correspondent
Published Wednesday, 19 March 2026, 07:55 GMT  ·  Updated 14:20 GMT

On the morning of 31 July 1917, Private Thomas Albert Webb of the 4th Battalion Royal Fusiliers went over the top at Passchendaele. He was nineteen years old. He had been in France for eleven weeks. He was from a terraced house on Leek Road, Stoke-on-Trent, where his mother kept a small grocery and his father, until his death the previous winter, had worked at a pottery kiln. The ground was mud. The rain had been continuous for three days. The German line was four hundred yards away and defended by machine guns, concrete pillboxes, and wire that stretched as far as anyone could see.

Thomas Webb walked back across that ground that evening. He was one of sixty-one men from his battalion who did.

"When I found the casualty rolls and saw his name wasn't on them — I had to check three times," says Graham Hartley, Head of Primate Care at Kestrel Valley Wildlife Park and the man responsible for what has become, improbably, one of the most talked-about archival searches of recent months. "I couldn't quite believe it."

"He went over the top at Passchendaele at nineteen years old. He came back. And then, somehow, his helmet ended up in a Bristol zoo."

— Daniel Phipps, History Correspondent

The search began in December 2025, shortly after Hartley identified the helmet as a British Mk II Brodie and noted the ink inscription on the interior rim. Working initially through the Commonwealth War Graves Commission's online records — which list the 1.7 million Commonwealth casualties of the First World War but do not, by definition, include those who survived — Hartley confirmed only that no Thomas Webb of the 4th Royal Fusiliers appeared on any memorial or burial register.

He then contacted the National Archives at Kew, where the surviving service records of First World War soldiers — many partially destroyed in the Blitz, the rest preserved in varying states of legibility — are held. A researcher there identified a service record for one Webb, T.A., Private, 4th Btn. Royal Fusiliers, Service No. G/14882, enlisted October 1916, aged eighteen.

The record was incomplete, damaged at the edges, but legible enough to establish the outline of a life: enlisted voluntarily at eighteen; trained at Folkestone; embarked for France in May 1917; wounded at Passchendaele — a shell fragment, left shoulder — and evacuated to a field hospital in late August 1917. Returned to active duty October 1917. Demobilised January 1919. Discharged to a home address in Stoke-on-Trent.

The 4th Battalion Royal Fusiliers suffered approximately 340 casualties at Passchendaele between July and November 1917 — roughly sixty percent of its effective strength at the start of the offensive. Of the men who went over the top on 31 July, fewer than one in three returned uninjured.

From there, Hartley — working with the help of a local historian named Rosemary Chell, who has spent forty years researching Staffordshire's First World War contribution — traced Webb through the 1921 census, where he appears living at the Leek Road address with his mother, occupation listed as labourer, pottery works. He married in 1923, a woman named Dorothy Ellams. They had two children. He appears in the 1939 National Register, age forty-one, by then working as a supervisor, Spode factory. He died, peacefully, in a Stoke-on-Trent nursing home in February 1978. He was seventy-nine years old.

"He had a whole life," Hartley says quietly. "He went through the worst thing imaginable and he came home and he had a whole, ordinary, long life. And somehow his helmet ended up in Bristol, in a zoo storage room, on a shelf above an orphaned baby monkey."

"He had a whole life. And somehow his helmet ended up on a shelf above an orphaned baby monkey."

— Graham Hartley, Head of Primate Care, Kestrel Valley Wildlife Park

How the helmet travelled from Stoke-on-Trent to Bishopsworth remains unclear. Rosemary Chell's best hypothesis — supported by local Home Guard records that show the Kestrel Valley site, then a working farmstead, as a designated equipment storage point from 1940 — is that it was donated, along with other First World War surplus equipment, as part of the wartime salvage drives that saw thousands of private artefacts pass into institutional hands.

"The helmet would have been twenty-odd years old by then," Chell explains. "A lot of men gave away their kit. Some couldn't look at it. Some just thought it was useful. We'll probably never know exactly how it got there. But someone brought it. Someone decided it was worth keeping."

Kestrel Valley has now made contact, through Chell, with Webb's surviving family — a granddaughter named Patricia, and two great-grandchildren, both of whom live in the Midlands. They were, Patricia told Chell in a letter relayed to the park, "completely astonished" by the story. They had known their grandfather served. They had not known about the helmet. They had not, until very recently, known about Pip.

Patricia has been in correspondence with Hartley. She has asked for photographs. She has asked for the helmet to stay exactly where it is.

"She said: 'He'd have liked that,'" Hartley says. "She said he was very fond of animals."

The helmet remains in its glass case in the keeper room at Kestrel Valley Wildlife Park. Pip, nineteen weeks old and recently introduced to his first troop companion, has not been formally introduced to it. But he can see it from his enclosure on clear days, Hartley says, if he climbs high enough.

"I'd like to think he knows it's there," Hartley says. "I'd like to think they understand each other."
