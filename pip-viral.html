Pip Hits 5 Million Followers: How a Baby Monkey Broke the Internet

It was forty-three seconds of a keeper talking about a helmet while a monkey watched him. Nobody expected it to work. It got fourteen million views in four days. Six weeks later, Pip the macaque has five million followers, a children's book deal, and a waiting list for zoo adoption packages stretching into the autumn.

MH
By Megan Holt  ·  Digital Culture Correspondent
Published Tuesday, 18 March 2026, 11:30 GMT  ·  Updated 16:45 GMT

Nobody at Kestrel Valley Wildlife Park expected the video to go anywhere. That much is clear from how it was made: Graham Hartley, the park's Head of Primate Care, filmed it himself on a personal phone with a cracked screen protector, uploaded it from the car park before he'd even watched it back, and didn't check his notifications until the following morning, by which point it had been viewed approximately three million times.

"I thought maybe the zoo's account would share it," he says, still appearing slightly stunned by events. "We've got a few thousand followers. Maybe. I thought it'd be nice for them."

The clip — forty-three seconds, handheld, no music, no captions — shows Hartley standing in the park's care unit with Pip clinging to his forearm. He tells the story of the Brodie helmet simply, in the same way he apparently tells it to anyone who asks: the storeroom, the stamp, the Somme, the incubator, the name. Pip, throughout, watches the helmet turning in Hartley's free hand with an expression that a significant portion of the internet subsequently described, in various phrasings, as "the most attentive look I've ever seen on any living creature."

Within seventy-two hours of posting, the clip had been shared by accounts totalling more than 200 million combined followers. It appeared on the feeds of nature documentary presenters, military historians, journalists, three sitting members of parliament, and — in what Hartley describes as "genuinely surreal" — a former England rugby international who posted it with the caption: not one dry eye in this household.

"I thought maybe the zoo's account would share it. I thought it'd be nice for them."

— Graham Hartley, Head of Primate Care, Kestrel Valley Wildlife Park

Social media analysts who have since examined the clip's spread identify several factors that contributed to its unusual reach. The video has no obvious hook — no jump cut, no trending audio, no text overlay. It does not conform to the rhythms of platform-optimised content. "It's basically just a man talking," says Dr. James Croft of the Oxford Internet Institute, who studies online viral phenomena. "Which, counterintuitively, is probably why it worked. It felt unperformed. It felt real. And the story it was telling — survival, connection across time, an animal and a piece of history — those are elements that tend to cut through."

The response has had tangible consequences for Kestrel Valley. Visitor footfall is up 340 percent compared to the equivalent period last year. The park's website crashed twice in January. The adoption programme — through which supporters can contribute to an individual animal's care costs in exchange for regular updates and a named certificate — has a waiting list stretching to late autumn. The park has hired three additional staff since February.

"We've had to be quite careful," says the park's director, Clare Whitfield. "We don't want Pip to become a performing animal. He's not here to be looked at — he's here to recover, to integrate, to have a healthy life. The attention has been genuinely wonderful, but it has to stay at arm's length from him."

Kestrel Valley Wildlife Park has declined offers from three television production companies to film a documentary series featuring Pip. The park issued a statement in February noting that it "will not permit commercial filming of any hand-reared individual during the active phase of reintegration." It has agreed, however, to occasional updates posted to its own social channels — a policy that has resulted in each subsequent video reaching several million views regardless of content.

The children's book — Pip and the Tin Hat, written and illustrated by Bristol-based artist Priya Nair — is due for publication in September 2026 through a mid-sized independent press. Nair, who contacted Kestrel Valley after the original video, says she was drawn to the story's layered quality. "It's a story about survival, but it's also about objects carrying meaning," she says. "About how something can connect you to someone you never met. Children understand that instinctively."

Hartley, for his part, says he tries not to think too hard about the numbers. He is, by his own admission, not very good at social media. He posts when he has something to show. He doesn't read the comments.

"I just want him to be alright," he says. "That's still the whole of it. The rest of it" — he gestures at something vaguely in the direction of the internet — "I don't quite know what to do with."

Pip, who has no social media presence and no verified account, currently has more followers than the Bristol City Council, the Natural History Museum, and the Royal British Legion combined.

He is, as of this writing, nineteen weeks old. He is doing very well.
